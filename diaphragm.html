diaphragm

The diaphragm is a major anatomical landmark that separates the thorax from the abdomen. It is a fibromuscular sheet that has a convex upper surface to form this delineation.
It has four major functions
Inspiration
During contraction the diaphragm increases the vertical diameter of the thorax . This decreases pressure within the thoracic cavity in order to draw in air. In expiration the diaphragm relaxes and elevates, creating the opposite effect in order to push air out of the body.
Abdominal Straining
The contraction of the diaphragm increases intra-abdominal pressure which aids in processes like urination, defecation, and child birth.
Abdominal Stability
The increase of intra-abdominal pressure during contraction of the diaphragm also increases the general stability of the trunk by minimizing displacements of abdominal contents, assisting the muscles of the anterior abdominal wall, and increasing tension in the thoracolumbar fascia thus stabilizing the spine.
Fluid Flow
The decrease in intrathoracic pressure during inspiration compresses the blood in the inferior vena cava to force it into the right atrium to fill the heart. This negative pressure also assists abdominal lymph vessels in upward flow.

Origin
The origin of the fibers of the diaphragm can be divided into three separate origin points

  • The sternal part originates from the posterior aspect of the xiphoid process.
  • The costal part originates from from the inner surface of the seventh to twelfth costal cartilages and adjacent parts of the corresponding ribs.
  • The lumbar part originates from the medial and lateral arcuate ligaments and from the first to third lumbar vertebrae.
